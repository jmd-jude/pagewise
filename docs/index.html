PageWise — Architecture & Proposal

The Precision
Gap.

Enterprise AI falls short for high-stakes document work. This is why — and what a different approach looks like.

The Problem

Fluent.
Incomplete.

Enterprise AI tools are genuinely good at drafting emails, answering questions about a document, generating meeting summaries. Legal and medical professionals have been early adopters, and for good reason — the use cases seem obvious.

For work that requires complete and traceable extraction from large documents, there is a structural problem. Not a catastrophic one. The output looks right, reads well, and is wrong in ways that only become visible when you check it carefully against the original.

When asked to produce a complete topical summary of a 250-page legal deposition, a capable enterprise AI model produced 60 entries. A page-by-page pipeline applied to the same document produced 77 — every one traced to a specific page range in the original.

The Constraint

Attention
dilutes.

Modern models can ingest hundreds of thousands of words in a single pass. The problem is not whether the document fits. It is what happens to the model's attention as the document gets longer.

A person reading a 300-page deposition for every substantive topic works page by page, building a running index. A model given the same 300 pages as a single prompt processes everything at once — and generates output that reflects its overall impression. Prominent topics, repeated across the document, surface reliably. Topics that appear once, briefly, in the middle of a long section, do not.

Liu et al. (2024) — "Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts," Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics — found that performance on information retrieval tasks degrades significantly when relevant information appears in the middle of a long context, even for models designed for long-context use.

Du et al. (2025), EMNLP — performance degradation begins well before context limits are reached. In some models, a large portion of the drop occurs within the first 7,000 tokens, far below stated capacity. The problem is not running out of room.
When It Matters

The threshold.

Short documents — under 50 pages — typically work reliably in a single pass. The risk curve steepens as document length grows. For expert witness and medical-legal work, most of the important material lives at exactly the lengths where the gap is largest.

Document Length Tokens Risk Implication
Under 50 pages < 25k Low Single-pass typically adequate
60–100 pages 30–50k Moderate Degradation begins; spot-check recommended
150–200 pages 75–100k High Systematic omissions expected
250–300 pages 125–150k Assume the gap 40–60% of substantive topics missed
Root Cause

Prompting has
a ceiling.

The natural response when an AI tool underperforms is to write a better prompt — be more specific, give an example, tell it to be thorough. For many tasks, this works.

For completeness over long documents, it does not. The model is not choosing to skip topics. It is producing the best output it can from a single pass over a very large input. Telling it to be more thorough does not change the underlying constraint.

Across multiple iterations in testing — with progressively more explicit coverage requirements, structured output formats, and quality checklists — coverage plateaued consistently at 40–60 percent of the substantive testimony in a 300-page deposition. The ceiling did not move because the instructions improved. It moved only when the architecture changed.

This is an architectural constraint, not a configuration problem. Better prompting will not close it.

The Solution

Architecture,
not query.

Let AI do what it is good at — reading a bounded piece of text and extracting structured information from it. Let software handle ordering, deduplication, completeness verification, and citation formatting.

Current State

Built.

This is a working proof-of-concept — not a proposal to explore an architecture. It already handles structured extraction and analysis across three workflows.

01
Deposition Summaries
Processes transcripts page by page, with each page examined in context of its neighbors. Assembles a complete topical index with page-level citations. Every page accounted for.
02
Medical Chronologies
Extracts diagnoses, medications, providers, and treatment decisions from medical record sets. Constructs a timeline with contradiction detection and gap identification. Each finding traced to source.
03
Document Indexing
Each document processed individually to extract structured metadata: date, type, page count, subject, key terms. Software assembles and sorts the index.

Each output can be reviewed, edited, and annotated by a practitioner before delivery. The system tracks what the AI produced versus what the expert modified — a distinction that matters for legal defensibility.
